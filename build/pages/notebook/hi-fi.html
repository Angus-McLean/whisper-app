Design evolution

Rubric : Three or more insightful and specific changes were made to the design based on feedback as well as on new insights from the designers. For every change made to the design from the point of the low-fidelity prototype, these decisions were justified from the perspective of usability or feasibility.

Based on the feedback we received (both from users and peer assessors), we decided to combine prototype #1 (seating) and prototype #2 (voice messages) to get the best of both worlds.

We also improved on the UI elements that caused confusion during the testing (e.g. unclear dots, confusing group display). Leveraging the interface from Jitsi, an open source video conferencing platform, we kept our UI close to traditional video conferencing platform so that most of the navigation will be familiar to the user.

Here are the specific changes we’ve made:

1. Combining Prototype #1 (seating) and Prototype #2 (voice messages)

From our 4 testings we did with our target users, we got mixed feedback on preferences. 2 users prefer the seating option, and 2 prefer the voice messaging option. All of them pointed out pros and cons of each prototype, and some expressed that both are cool features. Therefore, we have decided to combine both approaches for our computer prototype.

  • Justification in terms of usability and feasibility :
  • Valid use cases for both features at same time
  • Feasibility : opening audio channel between peers already, easy enough to send recordings?
  • Ease-of-use and added capability outweighs user’s cognitive and overall complexity of adding these features

2. Moving ‘Your Team’ into the chat bar and add a title

3 out of 4 of our users had trouble finding ‘Your Team’ in our paper prototype #1 and mistaken the participant list on the right as their group. To improve, we decided to move the display of ‘Your Team’ into the chat sidebar on the left, instead of having it floating on the screen. Unlike Zoom or Google hangouts, Jitsi has the participant list on the right, separated from the chat (which is on the left), so we decided to not reinvent the wheel and stick with Jitsi’s configuration. An additional title ‘Your Team’, and the same seating design should also make it clear that it is in fact, your team.

  • Justification in terms of usability and feasibility :
  • Increases usability and ease with which users can communicate with their team
  • Lowers cognitive load because they don’t have to navigate the participant list to find team members - increases speed

3. Click to record instead of click and hold for voice whispers recording

2 of our users are confused over click & hold, especially on a desktop setting. Therefore, we have decided to change it into click & record, and then click once more to send. The icon will also change from ‘voice recordings’ to ‘send’ when it is recording.

  • Justification in terms of usability and feasibility :
  • System should ‘feel intuitive’, wasn’t clear for users to click and hold, instinct is simply click on the button
  • Does increase the number of clicks needed to execute an action but doesn’t increase cognitive load or complexity so after consideration we’ve changed the interactivity of the feature

4. General UI changes

Most of our users are confused about the UI of both paper prototypes. In response, we have redesigned the interface (with Jitsi’s branding) to allow for a more seamless experience. For example, volume controls are no longer a pop up, and ‘End whisper call’ is no longer another pop up with checkmarks. Instead, both are displayed in the chat sidebar, with ‘End whisper call’ designed like a general ‘end call’ button.

  • Justification in terms of usability and feasibility :
  • Seamless, minimalism,
  • consistency helps minimize cognitive load

User population

Prompt : Does the described user population required for the test session correspond to the user segments defined in the project proposal?

Rubric : Description of user population required for test session correspond to user segments defined in project proposal

Our user population is generally people who use video conferencing regularly. We’ve segmented them into 3 ‘personas’, as written in our proposal.

The first are students, who want the convenience of collaborating with classmates; the second are young professionals in a dynamic environment who want to collaborate with close teams more quickly and generate many ideas. The last group are corporate multi-taskers who need delegation, coordination, and the ability to focus on the main speaker but remain able to quickly react to certain topics.

Usability Goals

At a high level we want the video conferencing system to recreate & facilitate the ‘sidebar’ interactions that humans routinely have in classroom and meeting environments (eg whispering comments or questions between peers). We believe these types of 'sidebar conversations' are an essential part of group meetings and collaborative effort. In order for a system to facilitate the collaborative effort and increase productivity rather than hamper progress with complexity and cognitive load the system needs to adhere to the following main usability goals :

Usability evaluation

Prompt : A detailed usability evaluation plan should enable independent evaluators to assess the design

with respect to the stated goals for the project.

Rubric : Complete and clear description of methodology to be used for the usability evaluation and the criteria to be accessed e.g. referring to specific heuristics from nielsen’s list

#1: Visibility of system status - The system should always keep users informed about what is going on, through appropriate feedback within reasonable time

Justification - user should be able to notice whether or not they are engaging in a whisper, or if anyone is requesting to whisper to them.

#2: Match between system and the real world - The system should speak the users' language, with words, phrases and concepts familiar to the user, rather than system-oriented terms. Follow real-world conventions, making information appear in a natural and logical order

Justification - e.g. use of words like ‘call’, ‘voice messages’ that are common. A ‘play whisper’ button that resembles commonly used button to play an audio

#3: User control and freedom - Users often choose system functions by mistake and will need a clearly marked "emergency exit" to leave the unwanted state without having to go through an extended dialogue. Support undo and redo.

Justification - User should be able to close unwanted menus. Close buttons are clearly visible to users

#4: Consistency and standards - Users should not have to wonder whether different words, situations, or actions mean the same thing. Follow platform conventions.

Justification - Interface design largely follows Jitsi’s which is of industry conventions.

#5: Error prevention - Even better than good error messages is a careful design which prevents a problem from occurring in the first place. Either eliminate error-prone conditions or check for them and present users with a confirmation option before they commit to the action

Justification - tooltip when users hover over whispering buttons is designed to tell them the purpose of each buttons because they are not common.

#6: Recognition rather than recall - Minimize the user's memory load by making objects, actions, and options visible. The user should not have to remember information from one part of the dialogue to another. Instructions for use of the system should be visible or easily retrievable whenever appropriate

Justification - buttons and controls are all visible in the interface.

#7: Flexibility and efficiency of use - Accelerators — unseen by the novice user — may often speed up the interaction for the expert user such that the system can cater to both inexperienced and experienced users. Allow users to tailor frequent actions.

Justification - in a real coded system, tooltips on hovering can disappear after X number of hovering assuming that the user is now familiar with the platform

#8: Aesthetic and minimalist design - Dialogues should not contain information which is irrelevant or rarely needed. Every extra unit of information in a dialogue competes with the relevant units of information and diminishes their relative visibility.

Justification - only information and buttons required to complete the action are present. Each pieces of element on the UI serves

a purpose.

#9: Help users recognize, diagnose, and recover from errors - Error messages should be expressed in plain language (no codes), precisely indicate the problem, and constructively suggest a solution.

Justification - no error message is needed because our use cases are quite simple

#10: Help and documentation - Even though it is better if the system can be used without documentation, it may be necessary to provide help and documentation. Any such information should be easy to search, focused on the user's task, list concrete steps to be carried out, and not be too large.

Justification - no documentation is needed because our system is simple
