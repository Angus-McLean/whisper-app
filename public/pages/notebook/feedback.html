Usability Testing Setup

Usability Test Objectives

Special attention to potential sources of error :

  1. Navigation errors - failure to locate functions, excessive keystrokes to complete a function, failure to follow recommended screen flow.
  2. Presentation error - failure to locate and properly act upon desired information in screens, selection errors due to labelling ambiguities.
  3. Control usage problems - improper toolbar or entry field usage.

Methodology

  1. Training, User Guides & Documentation
  2. Pre/post test questionnaires
  3. Test execution - Presenter and Participant
  4. Usability Metrics
  • Quantitative Measures : Completion Rate, Error-free rate, Time on Task
  • Additional Qualitative Feedback & Observations

As described in the documentation, the usability tests of Relier will be deployed to two user groups: speakers and participants in video-conferencing online presentations. Furthermore, each of our tests included 3-5 testers in order to correctly recreate the feel & interaction of video conferencing in large presenter-participant settings.

User Personas

Seeing as this system is a video conferencing app designed for large presenter-participant settings there are a couple of user personas we recruited as testers.

The types of users/testers we required match those described in the Relier usability test plan document and are as follows :

  • Participants
  • who are highly focused during online presentation
  • who are either shy or active in terms of participation
  • Presenters
  • who have some experience with giving presentations online
  • who really care about interacting with their audience and solicit participation

Usability Issues

From analyzing the results of the 6 user testings we did, we were able to pinpoint a few usability issues and rank them in order of importance:

  1. Unclear ‘You’re muted’ warning - High
  • This is an issue that appeared across everyone we tested with. It is clear that there is an error, but it’s not clear what exactly is the error.
  • Emojis are fun, but can sometimes be quite vague. Different users have presented different interpretations of what they think the emojis mean.
  • Screenshots :

  1. UI is too small - High
  • The text and buttons are too small to use comfortably. This will greatly hurt the user experience because users can’t see properly or click on something accurately

  1. High cognitive load - Medium
  • All of our test subjects took some time to stare at the screen before telling us what they think it means.
  • The large amount of information (with 2 moving graphs competing with each other for attention) will distract users from their main task, which is to either present or pay attention to the presenter

Test plan critique

Critique of usability goals

The usability metrics directly corresponds to the stated design goals. Completion rate, error free rate, and time on task relates to the ‘Ease of use’ and ‘Efficiency’ usability goal, and the qualitative measures relates to the ‘Efficiency’, ‘Effectiveness’ and ‘Satisfaction’ usability goals.

1. Additional observation on facial expressions

In order to better judge the testers’ emotions and actions in relation to the ‘Efficiency’ and ‘Effectiveness’ usability goals, where ‘not being distracted’ and ‘increase in engagement’ is judged, we propose to monitor the facial expression of the test subjects in additional to all the measurements proposed by the design team. Since ‘what they say’ might not be the same as ‘what they feel’, we hope to identify moments of ‘confusions’ or ‘delight’ on their faces as they were using the system.

2. No benchmark for Time on Task (TOT) of each task

Time taken to complete each task is measured to judge its ease of use and efficiency, but no benchmark on what is acceptable and what is not is given.

3. The usability goals gears more towards the Participants rather than the Presenter

In the post test evaluation for the presenter, ‘After asking a multiple choice question, I found it easy to assess participant answers’, ‘The question queue helped me organize taking questions’ do not really fit into any of the usability goals. We propose adding ‘Clarity & Organization: Software should help the presenter with keeping track of Q&A with the participant’ as one of the usability goals.

Also, it might help if the usability goals are separated into Presenter vs Participants, for ‘Effectiveness: Software increases engagement’ could be ‘The user should feel more comfortable to express themselves and feel more a part of the presentation’ for the participants, but for the presenters it might be ‘The presenter should feel like there’s an increase in engagement in the participants’.

4. Biased questions in post test questionnaire

All questions are positive statements, e.g. ‘I found it easy’, ‘I found it helpful’. This might cause biased results. Instead, wording it as questions like ‘How do you feel about expressing your emotions in a presentation’ with options ‘easier’, ‘about the same’, ‘harder’ will give a less biased interpretation.

Critique and modification of tasks

Some of the tasks include instructions that are ‘too explanatory’. Instead of asking the tester to use a certain function, we should ask them to accomplish a certain task and observe if they could find out how to do so.

Presenter:

Task 3: ‘Tell the user that the reactions from participants are coming to the reaction panel. Once the reaction panel is updated, ask the user to interpret the result of the reaction panel. Then, ask them to clear the result.’

  • Instead of ‘telling the user that reactions from participants are coming to the reaction panel, we will be telling the user that ‘participants are reacting on their end’ to avoid ‘explaining the system’, because a good design is supposed to be self-explanatory without the need of explanation.

Participants:

Task 1: ‘Ask the participant to add themselves to the question queue. Once the task has been completed, ask the user to remove themselves from the queue.’

  • Instead of ‘Asking the participant to add themselves to the question queue’, we will instead ask them ‘what would you do if you want to ask a question’? This will assess whether or not they could figure out how to use the ‘queue’ function without explicitly telling them to do so.

Design critique

1. Larger text and buttons sizes

Text should be of at least 16px to ensure readability. Buttons are also too small for easy clicking (Fitt’s Law).

2. Decrease the amount of information shown

There is a lot to take in at first glance ( 2 moving bar charts and a bunch of emojis). They also compete for attention so it’s not clear what should the user focus on. It might be a better idea to not show everyone on one screen, and instead have users expand certain sections if they need the feature. There might be other ways of simplifying it too, such as hiding the panel of emojis and instead have a drop down / search for reactions. Or, the emojis reaction frequency can be visualized as numbers (like slack reactions) instead of the bar chart.

The large amount of info needed to be processed on the side bar might also take the user’s attention away from the main speaker.

3. Room for error

There should be a way for them to undo the action if the presenter accidentally removed a certain person / cleared the entire question list.
